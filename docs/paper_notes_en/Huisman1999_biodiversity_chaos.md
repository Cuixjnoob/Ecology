# Huisman & Weissing (1999) -- Biodiversity of Plankton by Species Oscillations and Chaos

## Paper Info
- **Authors**: Jef Huisman, Franz J. Weissing
- **Journal**: Nature, Vol 402, pp. 407-410
- **Year**: 1999
- **DOI**: 10.1038/46540

---

## Background

The "paradox of the plankton" (Hutchinson 1961) asks why phytoplankton communities sustain far more species than the number of limiting resources would allow under the competitive exclusion principle. Classical theory predicts that at equilibrium, the number of coexisting species cannot exceed the number of limiting resources. This paper seeks an explanation from competitive dynamics itself, rather than from external disturbances.

## Methods

- Classical resource competition model: Monod kinetics + Liebig's minimum law
- System equations: dN_i/dt = N_i(mu_i(R_1,...,R_k) - m_i) for species, dR_j/dt = D(S_j - R_j) - sum(c_ji * mu_i * N_i) for resources
- Growth rate: mu_i = min(r_i * R_j / (K_ji + R_j)) over all resources j
- Numerical simulations exploring dynamics with 3, 5, and more resources
- Bifurcation analysis across parameter ranges

## Key Findings

- **3 species on 3 resources**: When species competitively replace each other in a cyclic pattern (species 1 best at resource 1 but limited by resource 2, etc.), the system produces sustained limit-cycle oscillations
- **5 species on 5 resources**: System can produce chaotic dynamics -- species replacement patterns never repeat; sensitive dependence on initial conditions
- **Supersaturation**: Oscillations and chaos allow far more species to coexist than the number of resources: up to 9 species on 3 resources, 12 species on 5 resources
- **Stable total biomass**: Under chaos, individual species abundances fluctuate wildly, but total community biomass remains nearly constant
- **Parameter range**: Competitive chaos occurs when species are "mediocre competitors" (intermediate parameter range in bifurcation diagrams)

## Key Concepts

- **Competitive exclusion principle**: At equilibrium, n <= k (coexisting species cannot exceed limiting resources)
- **Competitive chaos**: Chaos generated by competitive dynamics themselves, not external perturbations
- **Liebig's minimum law**: Growth rate determined by the most limiting resource

## Relevance to Eco-GNRD

- **Landmark paper for ecological chaos**: Directly proves that competition dynamics alone can generate chaos -- foundational theory for our project
- **Simulation framework**: The resource competition ODE provides a classic model for generating simulated chaotic ecological data
- **Severe information loss under partial observation**: Total biomass is stable but individual species fluctuate wildly -- observing only a subset of species means losing critical information about the system state
